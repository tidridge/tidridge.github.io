THE YARDLEY-JONES BENCH

 

 
 
 
 
 

 

 One wonders sometimes how things happen the way they do! Tidridge had noticed what he thought was a 'new' Memorial Bench, he investigated and found it was in fact an 'old' one where he had not visited the scene [shame] ... however, looking down the road to the west he noted a 'new' bench in the Henry Martell Park!

 The bench nestled in an open area of the park faces southeast. It has trees behind in and shade would be available in the hottest parts of the day. Traffic along Ada Boulevard can be viewed but the bench sits far enough back that the tranquility of the location would not be disturbed.

The bench is a memorial to Mary Yardley-Jones, late wife of the artist best known simply as Yardley-Jones. Fiona, one of Mary's daughters has promised more information.

 

The following biography was developed through the efforts of Fiona and Audrey, daughters of Mary. Thank you!

Our Mum, Mary Yardley-Jones (nee Kelly), was born in Trefor, a small village in North Wales. Since Welsh was her first language and an important part of her life, we chose a simple inscription for her bench in both Welsh and English.

Mum worked as a math's and science teacher in England and Wales before she emigrated to Edmonton in 1957 with her husband (our Dad) John. Together with their six children they lived in Edmonton, Toronto, and Montreal. During the late 50s and 60s, the family lived on 65th Street just below 112th Avenue (Mum taught Sunday school at St. Mary’s on 112th Avenue). Mum and Dad returned to the Highlands in 2006, so the bench’s location seemed perfect. Dad finds that it’s a lovely spot for a rest after coming out of the river valley or when walking along Ada Boulevard. We hope that others will enjoy this quiet spot and think of our Mum.

 Mum would never want a fuss to be made about her. But, she brightened many people’s lives. She volunteered with various groups across Edmonton including: Beta Sigma Phi, the St. David’s Welsh Society, the Edmonton Public Library, Girl Guides, and the Birkebeiner. She worked at the Royal Bank for several years (in Montreal and Edmonton) and was known to all the regulars at the Old Strathcona Farmers’ Market where she sold Dad’s artwork. She was a loyal Habs fan and an extraordinary knitter – she could watch a game, shout at the TV, and never drop a stitch. She was an avid reader, a whiz with crosswords, and made excellent martinis. But, most importantly, she was a lovely mother and grandmother who is greatly missed.

Mary with one of her 11 grandchildren, Liam.
