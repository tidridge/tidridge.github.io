THE 16TH CENTURY TITHERIDGE HISTORY

by Mike and Ann Titheradge.

Finding records in the 16 th century is difficult or two reasons. In many cases there are no longer any copies of the records and many of the records at this time are illegible unless you are able to read Latin or the strange script of Old English.

The earliest record we have found is a record of Ricardus Tytheryge marring Als Hall at Winchester St. Maurice in 1539.

We have also found a will written in Old English from May 5, 1542 of Richarde Tetridge of the parish of Chilcombe, Winchester. He leaves goods and his property in Avington to his mother Alys, his wife Margery, his son Wylliam and Margery's unborn child.

There is also a strange entry in the 1922 IGI "Tetridge Alys Mrs FS 05 May 1542 Chilcombe of" F= birth of first known child S=? (possibly stillborn) The entry does not come from the Chilcombe parish records, and its origin remains a mystery.

The exact connection between these three records is unclear although one would have to speculate there is on Richard with wife Alys, but why the wife is definitely called Margery in the will is strange and unexplained

.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

 

In the 17 th century an increased number of records with various spellings of the surname begin to appear. This shows the families enlarging and spreading throughout the small villages around Winchester. Unfortunately, most of these records from different villages cannot be connected to each other, although it is tempting to make some speculations. There are numerous parish records not covered by the IGI, but time has only permitted us to search a small number of the Hampshire parish records. This leaves many parishes we have not investigated at all, and it may be that there are lots of important Titheridge records just waiting to be discovered.
It should be remembered that some church records do not begin until the end of the century and large numbers of births, marriages and burials may have gone unrecorded and be lost

It would appear that our records in Avington connect to the earlier record of Richard, who in 1542 left land in Avington. The exact link remains unknown since the Avington parish records do not begin until 1610, leaving a gap of nearly 70 years.

 

 

 

The Cheriton parish records can be followed for 8 generations of Titheridges. The Titheridges of Cheriton move to other Hampshire villages such as West Meon, East Meon, Froxfield, West Tisted, etc. Thus the Cheriton family tree currently contains some 114 descendants of John and Ann Quallett. It appears to be the central tree from which many of the other branches of Titheridges are descended. IN addition to this central tree, we have found four other family trees that we had originally drawn up as trees in their own right, which we have now discovered are begun by the individuals who were born in Cheriton and are descendants of John and Anne. Unfortunately we do not have a piece of paper large enough to construct one giant tree for all these individuals! Altogether this gives us 368+ known descendants of John and Anne of Cheriton covering 12 generations and stretching up to the present day.
We feel sure that several more groups will eventually join on top this tree, once we can find the right pieces of information to make the connection.

Also we now know that Frederick born in Droxford in 1842 emigrated to Australia and produced a large family who spells their name Titheradge. Frederick joined the navy, spelling his surname Titheridge, but when he disembarked in Australia in 1880 wrote his name as Titheradge. At present we do not have the information on his descendants to draw up a tree but this will eventually give us even more direct descendants of John and Ann of Cheriton.

 

 
 

 

THE TITHERIDGE HISTORY 18TH CENTURY.

In the eighteenth century numerous other new family groups appeared in Hampshire, some of the most important ones, or the ones we know most about, are listed below. Many cannot be connected to earlier families.

In 1728 the Titheridges started to populate New Alresford. In 1737 William and Sarah had the first of their nine children baptised. Between 1737 and 1812 there were about 30 Titheridges born, but due to the uninformative nature of the early records, it has proved impossible to connect these individuals and families to other family groups and trees, except by speculation.

In 1744 Daniel and Rebecca Baker marry in Portsea St. Mary. It is the descendants of this couple who all spell their name Titheradge, producing one of the variations of the surname that survives today.
All the Titheradges that we know of are descended from Daniel and Rebecca, with the exception of one family group in Australia who are descended from Frederick mentioned above. Unfortunately we do not know where Daniel was born or anything about him. We would like to speculate that he came from Kilmeston and hence is a descendant from Cheriton, but we have no evidence at all for this , except that there is a Daniel of the right age born in Kilmeston. At present we are at a loss as how to prove or disprove this theory or how to discover his origins.
Hence our own direct family line remains stuck in Portsea in 1744, which is frustrating when we have made such progress with other branches of the family.

In 1761 William married Ann Cranston in Old Alresford. Their son, William produced a family of 7 children in New Alresford between 1784 and 1794 when William died. Whether he died of natural causes as suggested by the inquest report in the paper, or whether he was murdered by a neighbour, as later suggested by his son Richard, remains unknown.

The eldest child Robert, born in 1784, is possibly, the same Robert who married Lucy Ann Holdup in Gosport in 1808. One of their children, William Henry Walter, moved to London sometime prior to 1845 where he died a very wealthy gentlemen in 1886 leaving his descendants the considerable sum of 6189 (pounds sterling) in his will. He spelt his name Tytheridge as did all his descendants, so producing one of the two family groups who spell their surname Tytheridge. (We do not know how the other family of Tytheridges started other than they appeared in the London area around 1856 and then in Woolwich from 1878)

William and Ann's third son, Richard Cranston (b 1788) is perhaps the most colourful character amongst our ancestors and the one we know most about.
Our interest in this individual was attracted by a booklet in the Winchester Record Office entitled "Dickey Dung Prong or Richard Titheridge of New Alresford". The booklet is written by a minister who converted this rogue to Christianity shortly before he died in 1834 in Southampton.
Richard had a long list of sins to repent. He had been court martialled for deserting the army and for attempting murder, by attacking him with a dung fork, a soldier who tried to arrest him. This incident gave him his nick name. He had also been sentenced to imprisonment, transportation, flogging etc.
Among his list of crimes were theft, drunkenness, fornication and profound swearing. The booklet is fascinating to read but the story is obviously a little over dramatised for effect, although we have been able to substantiate some of the facts.

In 1768 the first Titheridges in Southampton are recorded, when John marries Mary Tucker at Southampton, St. Mary. They lived at Itchen Ferry with their family. John died a mariner who left a considerable amount of money and property (six houses) when he died in 1825. We do not know from where John came but it is possible that he came from Portsea.

Other small unconnected groups appear in other villages such are Farnham and Medstead.
 
 
 
 
 

THE TITHERIDGE HISTORY 19TH CENTURY.

The nineteenth century saw the Titheridges migrating further with family groups appearing in more Hampshire villages and also far afield as London.
Several families emigrated to Australia and New Zealand hence starting to spread the name world-wide. There were also many families still prospering where they had already settled such as in Cheriton, Kilmeston, Winchester, Alverstoke, Portsmouth, New Alresford etc.

We also know that George Cullimore Tidridge (b 1869 South Stoneham) emigrated to Eire.
Alfred William Tidridge (b 1871 South Stoneham) and Edward Harry F. Tidridge (b 1884 South Stoneham) emigrated to the USA.

The Tidridge variation of the surname appeared in the nineteenth century. Henry who was born in Bishops Waltham in 1815 and his wife Ann had eleven children. Although he was born as a Titheridge his surname became written as Tidridge, and this is the surname he passed on to all his children and future generations.

 
 
 
 

THE TITHERIDGE HISTORY 20 TH CENTURY

In the twentieth century the families spread throughout the United Kingdom, as it became both necessary and easier to move around to obtain employment. London and industrialised areas were obvious places to move to.
 

The Titheridges, like all families, suffered the loss of many family members in both World Wars. The number of children in the family has decreased throughout the century and certainly up to 1948 there is a reduction of births compared with the end of the nineteenth century. Also in many family lines the name of Titheridge is dying out due to the number of girls born.

Now there are many individuals who are intrigued by their unusual surname and eagerly tracing details of their ancestors and family history.

Mike and Ann Titheradge
