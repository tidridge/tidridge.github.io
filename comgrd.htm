How it started.....Lynn Spyker

I believe things happen for a reason and being in the right place at the right time helps too. It all started one day in 2008 when my husband Pieter was the President of the Highlands Community League when another member came to the house to discuss the up coming demolition of the long standing but deteriorating community hall.

They were discussing how much sod would be needed to cover the 7000 sq.. ft.. .. site. My ears perked up at that, having just been discussing community gardens with a friend that week. I interrupted with "Sod?? What do you want that for, you just have to mow it. How about a community garden?"

We pondered this for a moment then decided we had some time before demolition so why not look into it? So I did. I figured out how much soil would be needed, looked into grants with the City of Edmonton and gathered up a few like minded friends to create a core group and we got busy, really busy! Within 2 months we turned that idea into fruition!

We received a $5000 start up grant from the city which we used to buy lumber for the *raised beds, a shed and some tools and a sign for the garden. For less than the cost of sod we had some compost delivered and we got to work filling the beds. Everyone was so enthusiastic and the offers of help and free labor and expertise just added to the whole experience. It truly was a community event. Whatever we needed, someone new how to do it Word of the garden spread throughout the community and after holding our first registration day, we had a wait list! I believe the garden enhanced an already tight knit and amazing community. Other than losing a few veggies here and there from rabbits or passers by, we really didn't have too much other trouble in my 5 years of being the coordinator.

I am so happy that the garden lives on and that I had a hand in its creation. I loved living in Highlands, it was a very special place to live and be involved in. My family now lives in Victoria BC. where I am again part of a community garden but it doesn't feel quite the same. But I suppose harvesting veggies almost year round makes up for that loss :)
 

*the raised beds were not employed [corrected by Lynn] but a fence was erected

HIGHLANDS

COMMUNITY GARDEN
Where good things grow!

Our goal is to provide a space for those wishing to grow their own organic produce and share this experience with others.

We have 32 plots, one of which we grow for the community with produce free for the taking. The other 31 plots are rented out to individual gardeners. The 10 x 1 0 ' plots rent for $20 a season.

The 10 x 15' plots rent for $30 a season The previous year's gardener is given the option of keeping his or her plot, but there are always plots available to new gardeners each year.

Along with growing our veggies, we also promote the growth of a community spirit in the garden. We come together for work bees to set up the equipment in the spring and store it in the fall. We work
together to weed and mulch the pathways. But it's not a" work. This year we had a garden tea party, complete with good china cups, many of which were then turned into bird feeders.

We also had a good time at our sauerkraut bee, using the cabbages that grew in the garden.

And every fall we get together for a potluck supper - a fun, family oriented celebration.

We get support from SUSTAINABLE FOODS EDMONTON, who provided us with a bee hotel this year, as part of their project to study the prevalence of various solitary bees in the city.

We welcome visitors to the garden to join us in the amazing experience of seeing good things growing out of the good earth!

COMMUNITY NEWS

Oh, how our community garden grows

JAN KUPERUS

As I write this, the Highlands Community Garden lies blanketed in snow, and the hoses and water barrels are stored away for the winter. But hold on, things are happening there still! Have you noticed the compost bins? At our start-up meeting last spring, we had Mark Stumpf-Allen, the city compost guy, come to inspire us. We've made great strides thanks to gardener Trish Fontaine, who picked up scraps from Beverly Day care all year long. Trish has been working the bins right through winter, so that today we have real live compost, instead of our previous dead heaps of twigs and leaves. Coffee grinds provided by Mandolin Books and Coffee Company are really helping the piles to percolate!

You may be wondering why there are straw bales in the garden. This was an experiment that introduced some excitement and a little bit of wonderment into the season - as well as tantalizing scents of chicken feathers and fish meal, our organic fertilizers. When you fertilize the bales, they start to break down, and you can plant right into them, avoiding weeds and maximizing growth, because of the heat that is produced as they break down. This experiment was a qualified success. We saw some good cucumbers and squashes, and the tomatoes were doing great before the frost, but some of the bales didn't break down as well as others. The ones that are broken down will go into the compost, and we'll plant into the others next year.

You may have noticed that our community garden got a little sprucing up last year. One lovely spring morning, I was out on my bike and spotted some activity in the garden. It was one of our gardeners, Jean, and her grandson Seth, hard at it, scraping and painting our rather derelict benches and table a bright white, and then they completed the beautification with two pots of purple petunias. It was just the spot for a coffee break during the three work bees we had last year. We had two in the spring: one for setting up and weeding and mulching the paths, another to plant A carrot from Jenn and Holly's garden. the straw bales. We had another weeding frenzy in August.

Also new this year was the community plot, planted with carrots, zucchini, and tomatoes. This is a way to share with others what the garden produces; we prefer this way of sharing to the other - pilfering. We lost some of our beans and carrots that way. Then there was the case of the cut locks - two of them last year. We lost our lawn mower on the second break-in. The next day one of our gardeners showed up with a replacement mower, telling us it needed sharpening. Then one afternoon I met a fellow from Recovery Acres, who was mowing the weeds around the straw
bales. It was tough slogging with the dull mower, but he told me it was just what he needed; it was helping him to release pent up frustration! Later, another gentleman from Recovery Acres volunteered to sharpen the blades. So all's well that ends well!
