Also amazing are two ancestry events I've had recently.

1. a letter from Anne Brady in Auckland who told me the name of the ship our ancestors arrived on in 1874. and it's still a float , at a maritime museum in San Diego renamed the Star of India in 1898 after apparently called the Euterpe when built in the Isle of man in 1863.

She is planning to visit it in 2013 when the 150th year anniversary is, They still take it out for the occasional sail. AND ........... just tonight I had a phone call from a Craig Titheridge in Christchurch who is also in genealogy and has traced his family back to East Meon in Hants England but can't find the connection to us.

Our family, through POP's grandfather James was born in East Meon 1840 and he was on board in 1874 while POP's father Edwin was born in NZ (Featherstone) later that year (about 9 months after the ship arrived) However, Craig's information has a Sarah Titheridge arriving on a ship called the Peerless on 26/3/1874. I don't yet know WHERE it landed but it may have been Canterbury.

 John, I'm having another burst of enthusiasm as we approach a family reunion weekend to take our Father (Neil Alexander Titheridge's NAT Special - a homebuilt classic racing car) out for a spin around the local track.
 
 While going through notebooks to establish the history of the car, I've come accross your website and discovered a couple of things I think are inconsistent.

Edward (Ted) TITHERIDGE
27 Dec 1874 - 24 Nov 1946
BIRTH: 27 Dec 1874, Featherston,Wgtn,NZ
DEATH: 24 Nov 1946, Public Hospital,Chch,NZ
BURIAL: 26 Nov 1946, Bromley Cemetery ,Bromley,Chch,NZ
Father: James TITHERIDGE
Mother: Eliza ROSSEY

In the family tree it says Ted came to NZ some time before 1897 (when he married). This seems to imply he was BORN in NEW ZEALAND. My  information has him coming out on a ship between 1874 and that year 1897.

 The name Edwin is also associated with Ted in some documents, but as Edward was a more common name in those days and other boys were named Henry , James etc. I think Edwin is a mis-spelling.

I have tried to find ship's passenger lists and failed, but will renew my efforts to establish exactly when and where they landed.

Photo is of me in the NAT Special in 2008 , the day we bought it for the family. Same date as the Family photo Bruce sent you.

John, The Jenga game is universal , I thought. It is usually much smaller and played on a table with wooden blocks. Jenga is a game of physical and mental skill created by Leslie Scott,
and currently marketed by Parker Brothers, a division of Hasbro.
During the game, players take turns removing one block at a time from a tower constructed of 54 blocks. Each block removed is then balanced on top of the tower, creating a progressively taller but less stable structure. WHEN the tower crashes . the game is over. It can be very
tense and exciting near the end and spectators can get a kick out of it too.

The name jenga is derived from a Swahili word meaning "build".[1]

Each block is three times as long as its width, and one fifth as thick as its length 1.5 cm × 2.5 cm × 7.5 cm
Kendra wanted a GIANT set to be played OUTSIDE by wedding guests while waiting before and after the wedding, so I set about cutting and sanding the 54 blocks FOUR times original size. ( 6x10x30 cm ) It took me about 6 hours , largely by hand and $100 worth of quality laminated
wood - to avoid warping - and coated EVERYTHING in the garage in a fine layer of saw/sanding dust.
 

The smaller (commercial ) set starts out about 27 cm high and can get up to 60 cm before the thing topples. MY one starts at around a metre and can get up over 2 metres, which is pretty scary when the blocks are that big!!
